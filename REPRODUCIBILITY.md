## 1. Introduction

This document describes the complete experimental workflow behind the **MLCheM Selector**,
a literature-trained classifier that recommends a computational chemistry method
(COSMO-RS, DFT, or MD) from a short description of a target calculation. It is intended
to let an independent reader verify every reported result — the dataset, preprocessing,
model selection, held-out evaluation, per-class metrics, and confusion matrix — from the
materials provided in this repository. It directly addresses the reviewer requests for a
reproducible dataset, preprocessing and training scripts, a documented train/test split,
a pinned environment, worked example cases, version information, and an open-source
license.

## 3. Data Curation

The benchmark dataset was manually curated from peer-reviewed computational chemistry publications to provide a transparent and reproducible benchmark for computational method recommendation. Publications were systematically collected from major scientific publishers, including Elsevier, ACS Publications, Springer Nature, Wiley, MDPI, Taylor & Francis, the Royal Society of Chemistry (RSC), and other reputable journals covering diverse areas of computational chemistry.

Studies were included only if they (i) reported an original computational chemistry investigation, (ii) clearly identified the primary computational method employed, and (iii) provided sufficient methodological information to characterize the computational task. Review articles, editorials, perspectives, duplicate publications, and studies lacking a clearly identifiable primary computational method were excluded.

Each publication was manually reviewed, and structured metadata were extracted, including the computational property, sub-property, application domain, system type, primary computational method, secondary computational method (when applicable), and bibliographic information (DOI and paper title). The primary computational method reported by the original authors was assigned as the ground-truth label for supervised learning, while complementary methods were retained as descriptive metadata but were not used as target labels.

To improve consistency across publications, synonymous methodological terms were standardized through a canonicalization process. Although many studies employed multiple computational techniques, only the principal method responsible for the primary scientific findings was assigned as the prediction target. This resulted in a consistent three-class classification problem comprising Density Functional Theory (DFT), Molecular Dynamics (MD), and COSMO-RS, while preserving secondary methods as contextual information within the dataset.

The final benchmark dataset contains 323 manually curated records, each corresponding to a unique published study. By deriving labels directly from peer-reviewed literature rather than author-defined heuristics or synthetic rules, the dataset provides an evidence-based foundation for training and evaluating the MLCheM Selector.

## 5. Quality Assurance

Several measures were taken to ensure data quality and evaluation integrity:

- **Manual review.** Each publication was individually inspected and its metadata extracted by hand, following the inclusion/exclusion criteria in Section 3.
- **Label standardization.** Synonymous method names were normalized through a deterministic canonicalization rule (`canonicalize_method()` in `Model/train.py`), reducing labeling noise.
- **Leakage prevention.** Because a single paper can contribute several records, the data are partitioned by publication (group-aware split), so no paper appears in both training and test sets.
- **Deterministic pipeline.** A fixed random seed (`RANDOM_STATE = 42`) is used throughout, and the TF-IDF vectorizer is fitted on the training pool only.
- **Blind evaluation.** All reported metrics are computed on a held-out set of unseen publications that the model never saw during training or model selection.

## 7. Model Training

Each record's four descriptive fields are concatenated (`property . sub_property .
Application Domain . System Type`) and encoded with TF-IDF (unigrams + bigrams, 1500
terms, sublinear term-frequency scaling, English stop-words removed). The task is posed
as single-label classification. Nine candidate classifiers (Naive-Bayes variants,
logistic regression, a calibrated linear SVM, a soft-voting ensemble, and gradient
boosting) are compared using 5-fold Stratified Group K-Fold cross-validation within the
training pool. Rather than selecting the highest score alone, candidates within 0.5% of
the best cross-validation macro-F1 are shortlisted and the one with the smallest
train–validation generalisation gap is chosen — a **Complement Naive-Bayes classifier
(α = 0.5)**.

## 8. Evaluation Protocol

The model is evaluated on a blind held-out test set (50 records from 48 unseen
publications, 15% of the data). Metrics are classification-oriented and compared against
two baselines.

| Metric (blind held-out, n = 50) | Value |
|---|---|
| Accuracy | 0.880 |
| Macro-F1 | 0.877 |
| Top-2 accuracy | 1.000 |
| Deterministic rule-based baseline | 0.740 |
| Majority-class (Zero-R) baseline | 0.443 |

## 10. Computational Requirements

The pipeline is lightweight and runs on commodity hardware:

- **No GPU required.** Training and inference run on CPU.
- **Environment:** Python 3.9+ with the pinned packages in `Model/requirements.txt` (notably `scikit-learn==1.6.1`).
- **Training time:** on the order of seconds to a couple of minutes on a standard laptop CPU (the full nine-model selection loop is the slowest step).
- **Model size:** the trained classifier (`production_chemistry_classifier.pkl`) is ~150 KB; inference is effectively instantaneous.
- **Memory:** well under 1 GB.

## 11. Current Limitations

Although the benchmark dataset was carefully curated from peer-reviewed computational chemistry publications, several limitations should be acknowledged.

First, the current benchmark focuses on the three most frequently represented primary computational methods in the curated literature—Density Functional Theory (DFT), Molecular Dynamics (MD), and COSMO-RS. Many published studies employ additional computational techniques (e.g., TD-DFT, Monte Carlo, QM/MM, molecular docking, SCAPS-1D, and hybrid workflows); however, these are currently retained as secondary methodological descriptors rather than independent prediction classes. Future versions of the benchmark will expand the taxonomy to include a broader range of computational methods as additional labelled data become available.

Second, the dataset was constructed from peer-reviewed English-language publications that provided sufficiently detailed methodological descriptions. Although this criterion improves data quality and labeling consistency, it may underrepresent studies published in other languages or in venues with limited methodological reporting.

Third, each publication is represented by a single primary computational method, even when multiple computational techniques were employed within the same study. This simplification was adopted to formulate a well-defined single-label classification problem. Future work will investigate multi-label and hierarchical classification approaches capable of recommending combinations of complementary computational methods.

Finally, the MLCheM Selector is intended as a decision-support tool rather than an automated decision-making system. The recommendations are learned from historical methodological patterns reported in the literature and should be considered as evidence-based guidance. Final methodological decisions remain the responsibility of domain experts, who should consider the specific scientific objectives, computational resources, and problem characteristics of their individual studies.

## 12. Future Extensions

Building on the limitations above, planned extensions include: (i) expanding the method
taxonomy beyond three classes (e.g., TD-DFT, Monte Carlo, QM/MM, molecular docking,
hybrid workflows) as more labelled records are collected; (ii) multi-label and
hierarchical classification to recommend combinations of complementary methods rather
than a single primary method; (iii) broadening the literature base, including
non-English venues, to reduce sampling bias; and (iv) independent expert-labelled and
out-of-domain validation sets to further stress-test generalisation.
